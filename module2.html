01

Traditional Wisdom and Ecological Knowledge

Learning

Learning about the Nootka Rose helped me understand that plants are much more than part of the natural environment. They also carry cultural knowledge, history, and traditions that have been passed down through generations. I learned that the Nootka Rose is a native plant in British Columbia and the Pacific Northwest. It provides food and shelter for wildlife, and its rose hips, petals, leaves, and roots have traditionally been used by Indigenous Peoples for food and medicine. This learning showed me how closely people, plants, and the land are connected and reminded me that Indigenous knowledge is based on respect, relationships, and caring for the natural world.

Why I Selected This

I selected this learning because it changed the way I think about the plants around me. Before this assignment, I had never realized that one plant could have so many traditional uses and such an important place in Indigenous communities. Since I live in British Columbia, I wanted to learn about a local native plant and understand its cultural and ecological significance. This topic helped me appreciate Indigenous knowledge and reminded me that nature can be an important teacher.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

  • Provide opportunities to explore local plants through outdoor learning and nature walks.
  • Integrate learning about local plants across science, art, literacy, and social studies.
  • Include Indigenous perspectives in lessons whenever appropriate.
  • Teach students that plants are living parts of healthy ecosystems and communities, not just natural resources.
  • Encourage students to respect the land and care for the environment.
02

Traditional Indigenous Governance and Leadership

Learning

Through the ONECA Indigenous Leadership guide, I learned that Indigenous leadership is based on respect, relationships, and working together for the well-being of the whole community. I learned that effective leaders care for people's physical, mental, emotional, and spiritual wellness. They listen with humility, value the knowledge of Elders, take responsibility for their actions, and make decisions that benefit everyone. This learning helped me understand that leadership is about serving others, building trust, and creating caring and inclusive communities.

Why I Selected This

I selected this learning because it helped me see leadership from a different perspective. Before this module, I often thought of leadership as guiding or managing others. Now I understand that true leadership is built on respect, kindness, responsibility, and strong relationships. I was also inspired by Chief Dr. Robert Joseph and his commitment to healing and reconciliation. His work showed me how one person can make a positive difference by leading with compassion, courage, and respect.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

  • Model respect, kindness, responsibility, and humility in my daily interactions.
  • Build positive relationships with my students.
  • Create a safe, respectful, and welcoming classroom where everyone feels valued.
  • Encourage students to listen to one another and appreciate different perspectives.
  • Promote teamwork and cooperation through collaborative learning activities.
  • Encourage students to show kindness, empathy, and respect in their daily interactions.
  • Help students become thoughtful decision-makers.
  • Continue learning about Indigenous perspectives to respectfully include them in my teaching.
03

Comparing Worldviews

Learning

Through this learning, I gained a deeper understanding of Indigenous worldviews and the importance of our relationship with the land, water, air, and all living beings. I learned that Indigenous ways of knowing are based on respect, responsibility, reciprocity, and caring for the environment. I also learned about Two-Eyed Seeing, which encourages us to value both Indigenous and Western knowledge and use the strengths of each together. This helped me realize that students can benefit from learning through multiple perspectives and by building meaningful connections with the natural world.

Why I Selected This

I selected this learning because it changed the way I think about teaching and learning. I was especially interested in how Indigenous worldviews encourage us to see the land as a relative rather than a resource. I also found the concept of Two-Eyed Seeing meaningful because it shows that different ways of knowing can work together instead of being separated. This learning reminded me that education should help students develop respect for people, nature, and different perspectives.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

  • Include both Indigenous and Western ways of knowing in my teaching.
  • Provide opportunities for outdoor learning through nature walks, observation activities, and inquiry-based learning.
  • Invite Indigenous Elders or Knowledge Keepers to share their knowledge and experiences whenever possible.
  • Connect science lessons with Indigenous knowledge about local plants, animals, and ecosystems.
  • Help students understand that caring for the land is everyone's responsibility.
  • Support students in building meaningful relationships with the natural world.
04

Power of Indigenous Kinship

Learning

Through this learning, I realized that building a classroom community is about more than helping students get along. It is about creating a sense of belonging where every student feels respected, valued, and connected. I also learned that the Indigenous kinship model teaches us that relationships extend beyond people to include the land, water, animals, and all living things. These teachings helped me understand that learning should encourage respect, responsibility, gratitude, and care for both one another and the natural world.

Why I Selected This

I selected this learning because building positive relationships is one of the most important parts of teaching. I believe students learn best when they feel safe, included, and supported. The Indigenous kinship model gave me a new perspective on community by showing that caring relationships include not only people but also the environment around us. This learning inspired me to think about how I can create a classroom where students feel connected to each other and to the world around them.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

  • Create a classroom where every student feels welcomed, respected, and valued.
  • Take time to get to know each student's interests, strengths, and learning needs.
  • Encourage teamwork through cooperative learning activities and class discussions.
  • Provide opportunities for students to learn about Indigenous perspectives through stories and local knowledge, when appropriate.
  • Build positive and respectful relationships with all students.
  • Include outdoor learning experiences and nature observation activities.
05

What Is Your Connection to Land?

One place on the land that is especially meaningful to me is Green Timbers Urban Forest in Surrey, British Columbia. Life can often be busy with work, and family responsibilities, so whenever I visit Green Timbers, I feel like I can slow down and take a break from everything. Walking among the tall trees, listening to the birds, and spending time near the lake gives me a sense of peace and helps me clear my mind. It is a place where I can relax, reflect, and appreciate the beauty of nature. Every visit reminds me how important it is to step away from the busyness of everyday life and reconnect with the natural world.

Green Timbers Urban Forest is located on the traditional, unceded, and shared territories of the Katzie, Kwantlen, and Semiahmoo First Nations, who have cared for these lands since time immemorial. Learning about Indigenous perspectives has helped me see this forest in a new way. It is not only a beautiful place to visit but also a living space that has supported Indigenous communities for generations by providing food, medicine, shelter, and opportunities for learning. Knowing this has deepened my respect for the land and for the Indigenous Peoples who have been its caretakers for thousands of years.

Through this module, I have come to understand that land is much more than a place we live or visit. Indigenous teachings have helped me see that the land is a living relative that deserves respect, care, and gratitude. This learning has changed the way I think about my relationship with nature. Instead of seeing the land only as a resource, I now recognize that it supports all living beings and connects us to one another.

As an educator, I want to help my students build their own connection with the land. To achieve this, I hope to include outdoor learning experiences, nature observations, and opportunities to explore local plants, animals, and ecosystems. By encouraging students to spend time in nature and learn from Indigenous perspectives, I hope they will develop respect for the environment and understand the importance of caring for the land for future generations.

06

Overall Reflection

Module 2

Module 2 helped me deepen my understanding of Indigenous knowledge, leadership, worldviews, and the importance of building respectful relationships with people and the natural world. Throughout this module, I learned that Indigenous perspectives are based on respect, reciprocity, responsibility, and caring for the well-being of the whole community. I also realized that land, water, air, plants, animals, and people are all interconnected, and that learning can take place both inside and outside the classroom.

One of the most meaningful ideas for me was that Indigenous knowledge is living knowledge that has been passed down through generations. Learning about the Nootka Rose, Indigenous leadership, Two-Eyed Seeing, and the kinship model helped me see education from a broader perspective. I now understand that teaching is not only about helping students achieve academic success but also about helping them become caring, respectful, and responsible members of their communities.

This module also encouraged me to reflect on my own teaching practice. As an educator, I want to create a classroom where every student feels welcomed, valued, and connected. I hope to include Indigenous perspectives throughout the curriculum by using outdoor learning, encouraging inquiry, building strong relationships, and helping students develop respect for the environment and for different ways of knowing. I also recognize that learning about Indigenous perspectives is an ongoing journey, and I am committed to continuing this learning so that I can respectfully and thoughtfully include it in my classroom.
