Module 4

This module includes three key learnings and one overall reflection.

01

The Indian Act

Learning

This learning deepened my understanding of how colonial policies such as the Indian Act, the reserve system, residential schools, and the pass system have had lasting impacts on Indigenous Peoples and continue to influence Indigenous communities today. I learned that colonialism was not only about taking land but also about controlling Indigenous identities, governance, cultures, and ways of life (Canadian Research Institute for the Advancement of Women [CRIAW], n.d.; Joseph, 2024).

I also gained a greater appreciation for the importance of Indigenous self-government, language and cultural revitalization, and reconciliation (Crown-Indigenous Relations and Northern Affairs Canada, 2025). These resources helped me recognize that understanding Canada's history requires listening to Indigenous perspectives and acknowledging that the effects of colonialism continue to be experienced today.

Why I Selected This

I selected this learning because it challenged my previous understanding of Canadian history and helped me see the ongoing effects of colonialism from Indigenous perspectives. Before completing these resources, I knew about the Indian Act and residential schools, but I did not fully understand how government policies worked together to limit Indigenous rights and freedoms or how these impacts continue today.

Learning about topics such as the pass system, traditional governance, and self-government strengthened my understanding of reconciliation and emphasized the importance of respecting Indigenous knowledge, rights, and leadership. As an educator, I believe this knowledge is essential for creating an informed and respectful classroom.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

  • Integrate accurate Indigenous histories and perspectives throughout the curriculum rather than teaching them as isolated topics.
  • Use age-appropriate books, primary sources, videos, storytelling, and inquiry-based learning to help students explore the impacts of colonialism.
  • Incorporate Indigenous ways of knowing into everyday teaching and learning.
  • Invite local Elders and Knowledge Keepers to share their knowledge and experiences when appropriate.
  • Provide land-based learning experiences that help students develop meaningful connections with the land and local Indigenous communities.
  • Foster a classroom environment where students feel comfortable asking questions, reflecting on different perspectives, and engaging in respectful dialogue.
  • Encourage students to develop empathy, respect, and a commitment to Truth and Reconciliation.
02

Residential Schools

Learning

This resource deepened my understanding of the experiences of Indigenous children who attended residential schools and the lasting impacts these schools had on their identities, families, and communities. Through Irene's story, I learned how residential schools attempted to erase Indigenous cultures by taking away children's names, languages, traditions, and connections to their families (Dupuis & Kacer, 2016). At the same time, the book highlights the strength and resilience of Indigenous children and families, showing that culture, identity, and belonging remained important sources of hope despite these experiences. This learning reinforced the importance of teaching Truth and Reconciliation through authentic Indigenous voices and lived experiences.

Why I Selected This

I selected this resource because it is based on the true story of residential school Survivor Irene Couchie and is co-written by her granddaughter, Jenny Kay Dupuis, making it an authentic Indigenous resource. I believe students connect more deeply with history when it is shared through personal stories rather than only through historical facts. The book presents a difficult topic in a respectful and age-appropriate way while encouraging empathy, understanding, and meaningful conversations about Canada's history. It also aligns with the goals of Truth and Reconciliation by helping students recognize the importance of respecting Indigenous identities, cultures, and experiences.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

  • Use I Am Not a Number when teaching about residential schools, Orange Shirt Day, or the National Day for Truth and Reconciliation.
  • Encourage students to reflect on the importance of their own names, families, languages, and cultural traditions before reading.
  • Pause during the read-aloud to ask thoughtful questions about Irene's feelings, identity, and sense of belonging.
  • Facilitate respectful discussions that help students make connections to the themes of identity, belonging, and resilience.
  • Engage students in activities such as creating an Identity Shield or All About Me project to celebrate their own identities while respecting the identities of others.
  • Foster empathy, appreciation for Indigenous perspectives, and a deeper understanding of truth, respect, and reconciliation through meaningful discussions and reflection.
03

The Sixties Scoop

Learning

Creating a resource on this topic deepened my understanding of the Sixties Scoop and its lasting impacts on First Nations, Inuit, and Métis children, families, and communities. I learned that thousands of Indigenous children were removed from their families and placed in non-Indigenous foster or adoptive homes, often resulting in the loss of their language, culture, identity, and connection to their communities (CBC News, 2020; National Sixties Scoop Healing Foundation of Canada, n.d.).

I also learned that the effects of the Sixties Scoop continue today through intergenerational trauma, while many Survivors have demonstrated remarkable resilience by reconnecting with their cultures, families, and identities (Legacy of Hope Foundation, n.d.). As well, this learning reinforced the importance of listening to Survivor voices and understanding that Truth and Reconciliation involves acknowledging both the historical and ongoing impacts of colonial policies.

Why I Selected This

I selected this learning because the Sixties Scoop is an important part of Canadian history that is often less understood than residential schools, yet its impacts continue to affect Indigenous communities today. I wanted to learn more about how government child welfare policies disrupted Indigenous families and cultures and how Survivors continue to advocate for truth, healing, and justice. Creating a resource on the Sixties Scoop also helped me recognize the importance of using authentic Indigenous sources and Survivor stories when teaching about difficult topics. As an educator, I believe students should understand not only what happened during the Sixties Scoop but also how Indigenous communities continue to strengthen their cultures, identities, and families.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

  • Integrate learning about the Sixties Scoop into lessons on Indigenous history, Truth and Reconciliation, and identity.
  • Use authentic books, videos, and Survivor stories to help students understand the experiences of Indigenous children and families.
  • Create a respectful classroom environment where students feel comfortable asking questions, reflecting on different perspectives, and developing empathy.
  • Use activities such as the "Suitcase of Memories" to help students reflect on the importance of family, language, culture, and identity.
  • Encourage students to make meaningful connections to the experiences of children affected by the Sixties Scoop through inquiry-based learning and discussion.
  • Help students understand the ongoing impacts of colonialism while recognizing the resilience and strength of Indigenous Peoples.
  • Encourage students to recognize their own role in promoting respect, inclusion, and reconciliation.
04

Overall Reflection

Module 4

This module deepened my understanding of the lasting impacts of colonial policies on Indigenous Peoples through learning about the Indian Act, residential schools, and the Sixties Scoop. Although I had some prior knowledge of these topics, I did not fully understand how these government policies worked together to disrupt Indigenous identities, cultures, languages, families, and communities. These learnings helped me recognize the importance of listening to Indigenous voices and understanding Canada's history from Indigenous perspectives (Canadian Research Institute for the Advancement of Women [CRIAW], n.d.; CBC News, 2020).

Learning about the Indian Act helped me understand that colonialism involved much more than taking land. It was also about controlling Indigenous identities, governance, cultures, and ways of life through government policies (CRIAW, n.d.; Joseph, 2024). This learning strengthened my understanding of Indigenous self-government, cultural revitalization, and reconciliation, while reminding me of the importance of respecting Indigenous rights, knowledge, and leadership (Crown-Indigenous Relations and Northern Affairs Canada, 2025).

Learning about residential schools through I Am Not a Number gave me a deeper understanding of the experiences of Indigenous children and the lasting impacts these schools had on families and communities. Irene's story helped me see how residential schools attempted to erase Indigenous identities while also highlighting the resilience, strength, and hope of Indigenous children and families (Dupuis & Kacer, 2016). This learning reinforced the value of using authentic stories to help students develop empathy and a meaningful understanding of Truth and Reconciliation.

Learning about the Sixties Scoop helped me understand another devastating chapter of Canadian history that continues to affect Indigenous communities today. I learned how the removal of Indigenous children from their families resulted in the loss of language, culture, identity, and community connections, while also recognizing the resilience of Survivors who continue to reconnect with their cultures and families (CBC News, 2020; Legacy of Hope Foundation, n.d.). This topic reminded me of the importance of listening to Survivor voices and acknowledging the ongoing impacts of colonial policies.

Overall, this module has strengthened both my personal and professional growth as an educator. It reminded me that teaching Indigenous histories requires more than presenting historical facts - it requires creating opportunities for students to develop empathy, think critically, and learn from authentic Indigenous voices. As I continue my teaching journey, I am committed to integrating Indigenous perspectives throughout the curriculum, creating respectful and inclusive learning environments, and helping students understand that Truth and Reconciliation is an ongoing responsibility built through learning, respect, and meaningful relationships.
