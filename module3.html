01

Doctrine of Discovery

Learning

Learning about the Doctrine of Discovery transformed my understanding of Canadian history and its ongoing impact on Indigenous Peoples. I learned that the Doctrine was more than a religious belief – it became a legal and political justification for claiming Indigenous lands and denying Indigenous sovereignty (Canadian Museum for Human Rights, n.d.). Although the Vatican has rejected the Doctrine, I now understand that many of its underlying assumptions continue to influence Canadian laws, policies, and attitudes today (The United Church of Canada, n.d.). This learning helped me recognize that colonization is not simply a historical event but an ongoing reality that continues to affect Indigenous communities (APTN News, 2023).

I also learned the importance of viewing history through Indigenous perspectives. The resources challenged the idea of "discovery" by reminding me that Indigenous Peoples had lived on these lands for thousands of years with their own governments, cultures, laws, and relationships with the land long before Europeans arrived (Tree TV, n.d.; Canadian Museum for Human Rights, n.d.). This shifted my thinking and reinforced the importance of questioning colonial narratives and seeking multiple perspectives when learning and teaching history.

Why I Selected This

I selected this learning because it significantly changed my understanding of Canada's colonial history. Before this module, I knew very little about the Doctrine of Discovery or its influence on Indigenous rights and Canadian law. These resources challenged my previous assumptions and helped me understand how historical beliefs continue to shape present-day relationships between Indigenous and non-Indigenous peoples.

This topic also connected strongly to my role as an educator. It reminded me that teaching history is not only about sharing historical facts but also about helping students think critically about whose voices are represented, whose voices have been excluded, and how historical decisions continue to affect society today. I believe this understanding is essential for supporting truth, reconciliation, and respectful relationships.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

  • Use authentic Indigenous voices, resources, and literature to teach Canadian history from multiple perspectives.
  • Encourage students to think critically about historical narratives and question stereotypes and misconceptions about Indigenous Peoples.
  • Create opportunities for respectful discussions and inquiry-based learning that help students understand the ongoing impacts of colonization and the importance of reconciliation.
  • Foster a classroom environment where Indigenous cultures, histories, knowledge systems, and perspectives are respected, valued, and meaningfully included across the curriculum.
02

Colliding Worldviews

Learning

These resources helped me understand that history should be viewed through multiple perspectives rather than a single narrative. I learned that when only one perspective is shared, it can create stereotypes, misunderstandings, and an incomplete view of history (Adichie, 2009). Through these resources, I gained a deeper appreciation for the importance of including Indigenous voices and perspectives when learning about Canada's past. I also realized that Indigenous Peoples had well-established societies, governments, cultures, and knowledge systems long before European contact and played an important role in shaping Canada's history (CBC, 2017; Kinew, 2016). This learning reinforced the importance of thinking critically about who is telling the story, whose voices are represented, and whose voices may be missing.

Why I Selected This

I selected this learning because it challenged the way I had previously thought about history and encouraged me to examine historical events from different perspectives. The Danger of a Single Story reminded me that relying on one perspective can lead to inaccurate assumptions and stereotypes (Adichie, 2009). Combined with the other resources, it helped me recognize the importance of using authentic Indigenous voices and experiences when teaching Canadian history (Kinew, 2016; NCTR, n.d.). As an educator, I believe it is my responsibility to help students develop a balanced understanding of history by encouraging them to question sources, think critically, and appreciate diverse perspectives.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

  • Use a variety of authentic Indigenous resources, including books, videos, oral histories, and primary sources, to present multiple perspectives on history.
  • Encourage students to ask questions such as:
  • Who is telling this story?
  • Whose voices are included?
  • Whose voices are missing?
  • Use inquiry, discussion, and reflection to help students recognize bias and appreciate diverse perspectives.
  • Teach students that there is never only one story and that multiple perspectives lead to a more complete understanding of history.
  • Integrate Indigenous perspectives across the curriculum to support Truth and Reconciliation.
  • Foster an inclusive classroom where respectful dialogue, empathy, critical thinking, and every student's voice are valued.
03

Royal Proclamation

Learning

Learning about the Royal Proclamation of 1763 and treaties helped me understand that treaties are living agreements that continue to shape the relationship between Indigenous Peoples and non-Indigenous Canadians today. I learned that treaties were intended to establish relationships based on peace, friendship, mutual respect, and shared responsibilities rather than simply transferring land (Treaty Promises: Episode 1 – Treaty Making, n.d.; BC Treaty Commission, n.d.). One of the most important ideas I learned was that many Indigenous Nations understood treaties as agreements to share the land, while the written treaties often used words such as "cede" and "surrender," leading to very different interpretations of the agreements (Treaty Promises: Episode 2 – Interpreting and Understanding Treaties, n.d.). I also learned that the Royal Proclamation of 1763 recognized Indigenous Peoples' existing rights to their lands and established treaties as the process for building relationships with Indigenous Nations (Nation to Nation: Honouring the Royal Proclamation of 1763, n.d.; Justice Murray Sinclair on the Royal Proclamation of 1763, n.d.). In addition, I learned why the modern treaty process is especially important in British Columbia, where most of the province is on unceded Indigenous territory (BC Treaty Commission, n.d.).

Why I Selected This

I chose this as one of my key learnings because it significantly changed my understanding of treaties by showing that they are living agreements that continue to have legal, political, and cultural significance today. It helped me understand the different perspectives Indigenous Peoples and the Crown had when treaties were negotiated, particularly the difference between sharing the land and surrendering it. I also gained a deeper appreciation of the Royal Proclamation of 1763 and its importance in recognizing Indigenous land rights and establishing treaty relationships. Overall, this learning strengthened my understanding of reconciliation and reinforced the importance of honouring treaty promises by building respectful relationships and maintaining ongoing commitments between Indigenous Peoples and the Crown.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

  • Teach students that treaties are living agreements that continue to influence our lives today, not simply historical events.
  • Use treaty maps and Native Land Digital to help students identify whose traditional territories they live on and explore treaty areas across Canada.
  • Include Indigenous perspectives when teaching Canadian history, geography, and citizenship so students understand multiple viewpoints.
  • Encourage respectful discussions and inquiry about treaties, Indigenous rights, and reconciliation, helping students understand the importance of relationships, responsibility, and mutual respect.
  • Model respect by acknowledging the traditional territories where we learn and helping students recognize their shared responsibility in supporting reconciliation.
04

Overall Reflection

Module 3

This module has helped me develop a much deeper understanding of Indigenous histories, cultures, and ways of knowing. At the beginning, I had limited knowledge about many of these topics. Through each topic, I have gained new perspectives that will shape both my personal growth and my future teaching practice.

One of my biggest learnings was understanding the true purpose of land acknowledgements. I now recognize that they are not simply words spoken at the beginning of an event. They are a meaningful way to honor the First Peoples, recognize their enduring relationship with the land, and remind us of our shared responsibility to care for it and continue the work of reconciliation.

I also learned that terminology matters. The words we choose reflect our respect for people's identities, cultures, and histories. As an educator, I understand that using respectful and accurate language helps create an inclusive classroom where every student feels valued and respected.

The First Peoples Principles of Learning changed the way I think about education. They taught me that learning is built through relationships, respect, reflection, storytelling, community, and connection to the land. I now see that learning is not only about academic achievement but also about supporting the well-being of the whole person.

Learning about the role of witnessing showed me that listening is an active responsibility. Witnessing involves paying careful attention, reflecting on what we learn, and respectfully sharing that knowledge with others. This has encouraged me to create classroom opportunities where students listen thoughtfully, reflect on their learning, and value different perspectives.

The Indigenous Ally Toolkit reminded me that being an ally is more than saying the right words - it requires meaningful action. Being an ally means continuing to learn, listening to Indigenous voices, speaking up against discrimination, challenging stereotypes, and helping create an inclusive and respectful environment for everyone.

As I move forward in my teaching career, I am committed to continuing my learning journey. I will respectfully integrate Indigenous perspectives throughout the curriculum, build positive relationships with Indigenous communities whenever possible, and create learning experiences that help students develop respect for the land, Indigenous Peoples, and one another. This course has given me valuable knowledge and inspired me to become a more thoughtful, inclusive, and culturally responsive educator.
