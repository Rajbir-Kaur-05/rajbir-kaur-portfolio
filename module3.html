01

Doctrine of Discovery

Learning

Learning about the Doctrine of Discovery transformed my understanding of Canadian history and its ongoing impact on Indigenous Peoples. I learned that the Doctrine was more than a religious belief – it became a legal and political justification for claiming Indigenous lands and denying Indigenous sovereignty (Canadian Museum for Human Rights, n.d.). Although the Vatican has rejected the Doctrine, I now understand that many of its underlying assumptions continue to influence Canadian laws, policies, and attitudes today (The United Church of Canada, n.d.). This learning helped me recognize that colonization is not simply a historical event but an ongoing reality that continues to affect Indigenous communities (APTN News, 2023).

I also learned the importance of viewing history through Indigenous perspectives. The resources challenged the idea of "discovery" by reminding me that Indigenous Peoples had lived on these lands for thousands of years with their own governments, cultures, laws, and relationships with the land long before Europeans arrived (Tree TV, n.d.; Canadian Museum for Human Rights, n.d.). This shifted my thinking and reinforced the importance of questioning colonial narratives and seeking multiple perspectives when learning and teaching history.

Why I Selected This

I selected this learning because it significantly changed my understanding of Canada's colonial history. Before this module, I knew very little about the Doctrine of Discovery or its influence on Indigenous rights and Canadian law. These resources challenged my previous assumptions and helped me understand how historical beliefs continue to shape present-day relationships between Indigenous and non-Indigenous peoples.

This topic also connected strongly to my role as an educator. It reminded me that teaching history is not only about sharing historical facts but also about helping students think critically about whose voices are represented, whose voices have been excluded, and how historical decisions continue to affect society today. I believe this understanding is essential for supporting truth, reconciliation, and respectful relationships.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

  • Use authentic Indigenous voices, resources, and literature to teach Canadian history from multiple perspectives.
  • Encourage students to think critically about historical narratives and question stereotypes and misconceptions about Indigenous Peoples.
  • Create opportunities for respectful discussions and inquiry-based learning that help students understand the ongoing impacts of colonization and the importance of reconciliation.
  • Foster a classroom environment where Indigenous cultures, histories, knowledge systems, and perspectives are respected, valued, and meaningfully included across the curriculum.
02

Colliding Worldviews

Learning

These resources helped me understand that history should be viewed through multiple perspectives rather than a single narrative. I learned that when only one perspective is shared, it can create stereotypes, misunderstandings, and an incomplete view of history (Adichie, 2009). Through these resources, I gained a deeper appreciation for the importance of including Indigenous voices and perspectives when learning about Canada's past. Once again, this helped me better understand that Indigenous Peoples had thriving societies, systems of governance, rich cultures, and extensive knowledge systems long before European contact and played an important role in shaping Canada's history (CBC, 2017; Kinew, 2016). This learning reinforced the importance of thinking critically about who is telling the story, whose voices are represented, and whose voices may be missing.

Why I Selected This

I selected this learning because it challenged the way I had previously thought about history and encouraged me to examine historical events from different perspectives. The Danger of a Single Story reminded me that relying on one perspective can lead to inaccurate assumptions and stereotypes. Combined with the other resources, it helped me recognize the importance of using authentic Indigenous voices and experiences when teaching Canadian history. As an educator, I believe it is my responsibility to help students develop a balanced understanding of history by encouraging them to question sources, think critically, and appreciate diverse perspectives.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

  • Use a variety of authentic Indigenous resources, including books, videos, oral histories, and primary sources, to present multiple perspectives on history.
  • Encourage students to ask questions such as:
    • Who is telling this story?
    • Whose voices are included?
    • Whose voices are missing?
  • Use inquiry, discussion, and reflection to help students recognize bias and appreciate diverse perspectives.
  • Teach students that there is never only one story and that multiple perspectives lead to a more complete understanding of history.
  • Integrate Indigenous perspectives across the curriculum to support Truth and Reconciliation.
  • Foster an inclusive classroom where respectful dialogue, empathy, critical thinking, and every student's voice are valued.
03

The Royal Proclamation

Learning

Learning about the Royal Proclamation of 1763 and treaties helped me understand that treaties are living agreements that continue to shape the relationship between Indigenous Peoples and non-Indigenous Canadians today. I learned that treaties were intended to establish relationships based on peace, friendship, mutual respect, and shared responsibilities rather than simply transferring land (Treaty Promises: Episode 1 – Treaty Making, n.d.; BC Treaty Commission, n.d.).

One of the most important ideas I learned was that many Indigenous Nations understood treaties as agreements to share the land, while the written treaties often used words such as "cede" and "surrender," leading to very different interpretations of the agreements (Treaty Promises: Episode 2 – Interpreting and Understanding Treaties, n.d.). I also learned that the Royal Proclamation of 1763 recognized Indigenous Peoples' existing rights to their lands and established treaties as the process for building relationships with Indigenous Nations (Nation to Nation: Honouring the Royal Proclamation of 1763, n.d.; Justice Murray Sinclair on the Royal Proclamation of 1763, n.d.). In addition, I also came to understand why the modern treaty process is especially important in British Columbia, where most of the province is on unceded Indigenous territory (BC Treaty Commission, n.d.).

I chose this as one of my key learnings because it significantly changed my understanding of treaties by showing that they are living agreements that continue to have legal, political, and cultural significance today. It helped me understand the different perspectives Indigenous Peoples and the Crown had when treaties were negotiated, particularly the difference between sharing the land and surrendering it. I also gained a deeper appreciation of the Royal Proclamation of 1763 and its importance in recognizing Indigenous land rights and establishing treaty relationships. Overall, this learning strengthened my understanding of reconciliation and reinforced the importance of honouring treaty promises by building respectful relationships and maintaining ongoing commitments between Indigenous Peoples and the Crown.

How I Will Apply This in My Classroom

  • Teach students that treaties are living agreements that continue to influence our lives today, not simply historical events.
  • Use treaty maps and Native Land Digital to help students identify whose traditional territories they live on and explore treaty areas across Canada.
  • Include Indigenous perspectives when teaching Canadian history, geography, and citizenship so students understand multiple viewpoints.
  • Encourage respectful discussions and inquiry about treaties, Indigenous rights, and reconciliation, helping students understand the importance of relationships, responsibility, and mutual respect.
  • Model respect by acknowledging the traditional territories where we learn and helping students recognize their shared responsibility in supporting reconciliation.
04

Overall Reflection

Module 3

This module significantly expanded my understanding of Indigenous histories and the ongoing impacts of colonialism in Canada. Through learning about the Doctrine of Discovery, Colliding Worldviews, and the Royal Proclamation of 1763, I came to recognize that many historical events continue to influence the relationship between Indigenous Peoples and non-Indigenous Canadians today. These topics challenged many of my previous assumptions and emphasized the importance of learning history through Indigenous perspectives rather than relying on a single narrative (Canadian Museum for Human Rights, n.d.; BC Treaty Commission, n.d.).

Learning about the Doctrine of Discovery helped me understand that it was much more than a religious belief. It became a legal and political justification for claiming Indigenous lands and denying Indigenous sovereignty, and many of its underlying ideas continue to influence Canadian society today (Canadian Museum for Human Rights, n.d.). I also learned that although the Vatican has rejected the Doctrine, meaningful reconciliation requires more than symbolic actions. It requires governments and society to recognize Indigenous rights, respect treaties, and support Indigenous self-determination (The United Church of Canada, n.d.). This learning reminded me of the importance of teaching students to question historical narratives, recognize colonial bias, and value Indigenous perspectives (APTN News, 2023).

The concept of colliding worldviews helped me realize that no single story can fully represent Canada's history. Through these resources, I learned that Indigenous Peoples had well-developed societies, governments, cultures, and knowledge systems long before European contact, yet these perspectives have often been overlooked in traditional history education (CBC, 2017; Kinew, 2016). Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie's message about the danger of a single story reinforced the importance of examining history from multiple perspectives and recognizing whose voices are represented and whose are missing (Adichie, 2009). This learning strengthened my commitment to creating opportunities for students to think critically, question sources, and appreciate diverse perspectives.

Learning about the Royal Proclamation of 1763 and treaties helped me understand that treaties are living agreements that continue to shape relationships between Indigenous Peoples and the Crown. I learned that many Indigenous Nations understood treaties as agreements to share the land and build relationships, while the written treaties often reflected a different understanding through terms such as "cede" and "surrender" (Treaty Promises: Episode 2 – Interpreting and Understanding Treaties, n.d.). I also gained a deeper understanding of the importance of the Royal Proclamation in recognizing Indigenous land rights and establishing treaty relationships, as well as why modern treaties continue to be especially significant in British Columbia, where most of the province is on unceded Indigenous territory (BC Treaty Commission, n.d.; Nation to Nation: Honouring the Royal Proclamation of 1763, n.d.).

Overall, this module has strengthened both my personal and professional growth as an educator. It has shown me that teaching Indigenous histories requires more than presenting historical facts; it requires creating opportunities for students to explore multiple perspectives, question colonial narratives, and learn from authentic Indigenous voices. As I continue my teaching journey, I am committed to integrating Indigenous perspectives throughout the curriculum, using authentic resources, and creating respectful learning environments where students feel encouraged to ask questions, reflect critically, and develop empathy. My goal is to help students understand that Truth and Reconciliation is an ongoing responsibility that begins with education, respectful relationships, and a willingness to continue learning.
